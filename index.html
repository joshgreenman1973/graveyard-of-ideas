How to read this

Source attributes each entry to the most direct provenance available — the candidate's published platform where it survives, the relevant biography or memoir, contemporaneous press coverage, or Wikipedia entries that themselves cite primary materials. Where only secondary press coverage exists for a contemporary entry (2009-2025 candidates), that is noted.

Categorization follows the report's four-chapter taxonomy: won (the idea became law in some form), ahead (still in serious circulation, not yet enacted), bad (the prescription was wrong, not just the politics), strange (in a category of its own).

Fate is the more granular cut: adopted means meaningfully implemented in some form; alive means in active policy debate or partial implementation; buried means decisively rejected with no current movement.

What this is not. This is not a complete record. Several losing campaigns — Dinkins 1993, Ferrer 2001 and 2005, Thompson 2013 — produced platforms that haven't been mined here. Reader corrections welcome.

What this tells us

Four patterns from the graveyard

Losing candidates have often been better thinkers than the winners.

Hillquit, Thomas, Buckley, Mailer-Breslin, Abzug, Green: people with serious, articulated worldviews who lost to candidates whose appeal was machine-based, charismatic, fiscal-emergency-oriented, or self-funded. Voters don't pick mayors based on idea density. The ideas keep coming back anyway.

Most "weird" ideas turn out to be early.

Bike lanes were weird in 1965. Decriminalization was weird in 1965. Public housing was weird in 1917. Civil rights ordinances were weird in 1977. The category of "obviously crazy mayoral idea" should be regarded with some humility.

Some "weird" ideas are actually weird.

Sweet Sunday was operationally impossible. Mandatory civil commitment for addicts is morally indefensible. Banning private cars from Manhattan in 1969 with no replacement infrastructure was a thought experiment, not a plan. The category of "actually unworkable" is also real.

The line between the two is implementation, not ideology.

Hearst was right about utility capture and mostly wrong about whether municipal ownership would have improved things. Buckley was right about narcotics policy and wrong about what to do with addicts. The diagnosis tends to be more durable than the prescription, and voters should be more skeptical of the second than the first.

Methodology, scope and sources

Scope

"Failed" here means lost a general election or a major-party primary, between 1886 and 2025. Henry George ran in 1886 — pre-consolidation — but he is the godfather of the genre and is included. Candidates who later won (Bill de Blasio's pre-K plank, which Norman Thomas first proposed in 1932) appear when the idea, not the eventual victor, is the unit of analysis.

Categorization

Four chapters: eventually won (the idea became law in some form), ahead of their time (still in serious circulation, not yet enacted), probably bad (the implementation was wrong, not just the politics), and truly strange (in a category of their own). The "fate" filter — adopted, alive, buried — is a more granular version of the same axis.

Sources

Drawn from the Gotham Center for New York City History, City Journal, Manhattan Institute, Jacobin, the Forward, the New-York Historical Society, the LaGuardia and Wagner Archives, Skynomics Blog, and contemporaneous press. Specific candidate platforms were cross-checked against multiple accounts where available.

A note on what's missing

This is not a complete record. David Dinkins's 1993 reelection bid, Mark Green's 1997 and 1998 races for higher office, the full 2017 primary field beyond Sal Albanese, and Whitney Tilson's deeper education-policy planks all sit just outside what's catalogued here. Reader suggestions welcome.
